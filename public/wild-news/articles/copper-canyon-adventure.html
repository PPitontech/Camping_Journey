Exploring Copper Canyon: A Journey Through Mexico's Hidden Wonder

By Maria Rodriguez
Published on July 10, 2025
8 min read

The first rays of sunlight pierce through the morning mist, revealing a landscape so vast and dramatic it seems to belong to another world. I stand at the edge of a precipice, my breath caught in my throat, as the full majesty of Mexico's Copper Canyon unfolds before me. This is not just another hiking trip—it's a journey into the soul of one of North America's most spectacular yet underappreciated natural wonders.

The Call of the Canyon

When most travelers think of canyons, their minds immediately drift to the Grand Canyon in Arizona. But here, in the northwestern state of Chihuahua, lies a system of six distinct canyons that together form a network four times larger and in places twice as deep as its famous northern neighbor. The locals call it "Barrancas del Cobre"—Copper Canyon—named for the distinctive copper-green coloration of its walls.

My journey began three days ago in the colonial town of El Fuerte, where I boarded the famous Chihuahua al Pacífico Railway. This engineering marvel, completed in 1961 after decades of construction, traverses some of the most challenging terrain in Mexico, passing through 86 tunnels and crossing 37 bridges as it climbs from sea level to an altitude of 2,400 meters.

As the train climbed higher into the Sierra Madre Occidental mountains, the landscape transformed dramatically. Tropical lowlands gave way to pine forests, and eventually, the first glimpses of the canyon system appeared—vast, yawning abysses carved by six rivers that eventually converge into the Rio Fuerte before emptying into the Gulf of California.

Essential Gear for Canyon Country

Preparing for Copper Canyon requires careful consideration. The dramatic elevation changes mean you'll experience multiple climate zones in a single day. My WindTech Ultralight Backpack proved invaluable, offering enough capacity for all my essentials while remaining comfortable during long hikes.

My Canyon Essentials:

  • WindTech Ultralight Backpack - Perfect balance of weight, durability, and organization
  • Carbon Fiber Trekking Poles - Crucial for steep descents into the canyon
  • Portable Water Filter - Water sources are available but must be filtered
  • All-Terrain Hiking Boots - The rocky terrain demands solid ankle support
  • Sun Protection Kit - The canyon amplifies sun exposure dramatically

The dramatic temperature swings between day and night caught me by surprise. During midday hikes, temperatures soared above 30°C (86°F), while nights in the highlands dropped below 5°C (41°F). My 3-in-1 Convertible Jacket proved to be one of my wisest investments.

Meeting the Rarámuri: The Canyon's Guardians

Perhaps the most profound aspect of my journey was encountering the Rarámuri (also known as Tarahumara) people, who have inhabited these canyons for centuries. Renowned for their exceptional long-distance running abilities, the Rarámuri live in small, scattered settlements throughout the canyon system, maintaining many of their traditional ways despite the encroachment of modern society.

On my second day of hiking, I was fortunate enough to be invited to share a meal with a Rarámuri family. Their home, a simple wooden structure built into a cave on the canyon wall, offered protection from the elements while providing breathtaking views of the surrounding landscape. We communicated through a mixture of basic Spanish and gestures, sharing stories and laughter as we ate a simple meal of beans, corn tortillas, and locally grown vegetables.

What struck me most was their profound connection to this harsh yet beautiful environment. Every aspect of their lives—from their distinctive running culture to their sustainable agricultural practices—has evolved in harmony with the canyon's unique challenges and opportunities.

"We don't run to compete. We run because that's how we've always moved through our world. The canyons are vast, and our communities are scattered. Running is our way of connecting."
— Manuel, Rarámuri elder

This philosophy—of moving through the world with purpose and respect—resonated deeply with me. In our modern rush to conquer landscapes through extreme sports and adrenaline-fueled adventures, we often miss the deeper connection that comes from simply being present in a place, learning its rhythms, and moving through it with intention.

The Adventure Continues

As I write this from the small town of Creel, perched on the canyon rim at 2,340 meters above sea level, I'm preparing for the next leg of my journey—a three-day trek into Urique Canyon, the deepest of the six canyons in the system. The trail will descend nearly 1,500 meters to the canyon floor, where the small village of Urique awaits.

The journey promises to be challenging, with steep switchbacks and exposed sections that will test both my physical endurance and my mental resilience. But after my experiences over the past few days, I feel prepared—not just because of my gear or physical conditioning, but because I've begun to understand the canyon's rhythms and respect its immense power.

Copper Canyon isn't just a destination; it's a teacher. It demands that you slow down, pay attention, and approach it with humility. In return, it offers lessons in resilience, beauty, and the profound connection between landscape and culture that has shaped human experience for millennia.

As the sun sets over the canyon, painting its walls in shades of gold and copper, I'm reminded of why we seek these wild places. Not to conquer them, but to be transformed by them—to return to our everyday lives carrying a piece of their wisdom and wonder within us.

Stay tuned for the next installment of this journey, where I'll share my experiences from the depths of Urique Canyon and the lessons learned along the way.

Maria Rodriguez

Adventure photographer and writer with a passion for cultural immersion. Maria has explored remote regions across Latin America, documenting traditional communities and their relationship with the natural world.
